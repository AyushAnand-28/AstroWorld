Aurora Borealis: Northern Lights

The Northern Lights, or Aurora Borealis, are a natural light display that occurs in high-latitude regions, typically near the Arctic Circle. This mesmerizing phenomenon is caused by the interaction between solar wind—charged particles ejected from the Sun—and Earth’s magnetic field and atmosphere.

Meteor Shower: Shooting Stars

A meteor shower is often referred to as a display of "shooting stars," though they aren't stars at all! These brilliant streaks of light are caused by tiny pieces of space debris burning up as they enter Earth's atmosphere.

Lunar Eclipse

A lunar eclipse is a celestial event that occurs when the Earth aligns precisely between the Sun and the Moon, causing Earth's shadow to fall on the Moon. This phenomenon can only happen during a full moon when the Sun, Earth, and Moon are in a straight line or nearly so.

Solar Eclipse: Ring of Fire

A solar eclipse occurs when the Moon passes directly between the Earth and the Sun, casting a shadow on Earth and temporarily blocking the Sun's light. This celestial event can only happen during a new moon when the Sun, Moon, and Earth are aligned.

Supernova: Star Death

A supernova is a powerful and explosive event that occurs at the end of a star's life cycle, resulting in a dramatic increase in brightness followed by a gradual fading. Supernovae release an immense amount of energy, making them some of the most luminous events in the universe.
